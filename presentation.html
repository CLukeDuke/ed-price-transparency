Why does this gap exist?

The data is public — but unusable

Since 2021, CMS requires all hospitals to publish machine-readable files (MRFs) of every charge under 45 CFR Part 180

But for a typical patient these files are:

  • Up to 2 GB per hospital
  • Full of cryptic codes: CPT 84484
  • Impossible to compare across hospitals
  • No plain-language explanations

Who this tool is for

  • Uninsured patients choosing between EDs on cost
  • Underinsured patients with high deductibles
  • Anyone who wants to understand their bill before or after a visit
  • Health policy researchers studying price variation in Tampa Bay

Treatment definitions

The problem within the problem:

Even if a patient finds the price data, they still see CPT 84484 — Troponin I and have no idea what it means.

The solution:

Every billing code badge and service line item has a hover tooltip with:

  1. What it is — full name
  2. What it does — plain-language description
  3. Why you see this — why it appears on an ED bill

Example — Troponin I:

Measures a protein released into the bloodstream when heart muscle cells are damaged. Elevated levels strongly indicate a heart attack. Results typically return in 1–2 hours.

Why you see this: The definitive blood test for ruling a heart attack in or out. Cannot be skipped for a serious chest pain workup — you may be billed for it twice if a repeat draw is ordered.

Key design decisions and trade-offs

Decision 1: Static data + live Claude API

What we chose:

Pre-process MRF data into a static JSON file via a weekly pipeline. Call Claude API only for unknown symptoms at query time.

Why:

  • Hospital MRF files are 100MB–2GB each
  • Downloading 7 files on every search would be unusably slow
  • Pre-processing keeps response time under 1 second
  • Claude handles the long tail of uncommon symptoms

The trade-off:

Prices are up to 7 days old rather than real-time.

For hospital pricing data that is updated annually, this is acceptable — hospital prices don’t change week to week.

What we gave up:

Real-time MRF fetching would guarantee prices are always current. A production version with server-side caching could bridge this gap.

Decision 2: Payer multipliers instead of raw MRF payer data

What we chose:

Apply payer-specific multipliers to the cash price rather than parsing the full payer-by-plan rates from each MRF.

get_payer_price <- function(base_cash, hosp_id, payer_id) {
  if (payer_id == "cash") return(base_cash)
  multiplier <- PAYER_MULTIPLIERS[[hosp_id]][[payer_id]]
  round(base_cash * multiplier)
}

Why:

Each MRF contains hundreds of inconsistent plan name variations per insurer across hospitals — normalizing these requires a separate NLP classification step.

The trade-off:

Multipliers give clinically reasonable estimates but are not exact. The disclaimer on every results page is essential.

What we gave up:

Exact negotiated rates per plan — the most valuable data in the MRF.

Decision 3: Dot plot and treemap over bar charts

Why dot plot:

Bars imply the area matters — for prices, only the endpoint matters. The dot plot shows both cash price and gross charge with a connector line, making the gap between them the visual story.

Why treemap:

For abdominal pain, the CT scan is 60% of the total bill. A stacked bar obscures this. The treemap makes the CT scan rectangle dominate visually — the insight is immediate.

The trade-off:

Dot plots are less familiar to general audiences than bar charts. Some users may need a moment to understand the encoding.

Solution:

We kept both chart types toggleable via a radio button. Users who prefer bar charts can switch — the dot plot is the default because it tells a better story.

What we would improve with more time

Improvement 1: Real payer-specific rates

What we have now:

Multipliers applied to cash price — reasonable estimates based on Florida market data

What we would build:

A proper MRF payer parsing pipeline that uses Claude to classify inconsistent plan names into standard insurer categories, then stores actual negotiated rates by insurer and hospital.

Why it matters:

The difference between “estimated UnitedHealthcare rate” and “actual UnitedHealthcare PPO negotiated rate at Tampa General” is the difference between a useful tool and a definitive one.

Example of the problem:

Tampa General’s MRF contains plan entries like:

United Healthcare Choice Plus
UHC Choice
UnitedHealthcare Options PPO
UHC Navigate HMO
United Choice Navigator

All referring to UnitedHealthcare but stored inconsistently. Claude could classify these reliably — we just ran out of time to build the pipeline.

Improvement 2: Driving time + urgent care comparison

Driving time

What we have: straight-line Haversine distance from ZIP centroid

What we would build: Google Maps Distance Matrix API returning driving time in minutes — far more relevant for emergency care

“8 miles” means nothing in an emergency. “14 minutes” is actionable.

Why we didn’t: requires Google Maps API key with billing enabled — cost risk for a class project.

Urgent care comparison

Many ED conditions can be treated at urgent care for a fraction of the cost:

Condition ED cash Urgent care

The most impactful price transparency intervention is “should you go to an ED at all?”
